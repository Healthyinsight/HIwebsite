You go to the gym, lift the same weights for the same number of reps, and wonder why your progress has stalled. Doing the exact same routine week after week is great for maintenance, but if you want to grow stronger, your body needs a reason to adapt.

## Here's the thing

Progressive overload is the fundamental law of physical adaptation. To keep making progress without hitting a plateau, you must systematically increase the stimulus placed on your body over time, forcing it to adapt to new levels of stress.

## Here's what to do

**Prioritize rep progression first:** Before increasing weight, aim to perform more repetitions with your current weight. Once you hit the top of your rep range (e.g., 3 sets of 10), it is time to progress the load.

**Use micro-loading:** When adding weight, use the smallest increments possible. Increases of 1–2.5% (like using 0.5 kg plates) are much more sustainable than jumping 5 kg at a time.

**Autoregulate with RPE:** Some days you feel strong, other days you don't. Use Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE 1–10) to guide your intensity, ensuring you are pushing close to failure (RPE 7–8) rather than sticking blindly to a percentage of your max.

**Do not ignore volume:** Overload isn't just about weight. Adding an extra set or increasing the frequency of your workouts are highly effective ways to progress.

## What the research shows

Recent studies confirm that progressing reps or progressing loads produce similar strength and hypertrophy gains over an 8-week period. Furthermore, autoregulating your training using RPE is just as effective as traditional percentage-based training, making it an excellent and flexible tool for both beginners and experienced lifters. Systematic reviews also show that small increments are highly effective for sustainable progression, while overall progression protocols work equally well as long as volume and effort are consistently increased.

## Why it works

When you lift weights, you create micro-tears in your muscle fibers and stress your central nervous system. During recovery, your body rebuilds the tissue slightly stronger than before to handle that specific stress better next time. This is known as the general adaptation syndrome.

If you don't increase the stress — the load, reps, or volume — your body simply maintains its current state because the stimulus is no longer a threat. Micro-loading and rep progression ensure that the stimulus is always just slightly above what your body is currently capable of, forcing continuous adaptation without overwhelming your recovery capacity. Using RPE ensures that this stress matches your body's actual readiness on any given day, preventing overtraining.

## Avoid these mistakes

- **Rushing the weight increase:** Ego lifting leads to form breakdown and injury. If you cannot lift the new weight with the exact same form as the old weight, it is too heavy.
- **Ignoring non-weight progression:** Improving your technique, slowing down the eccentric (lowering) phase, or reducing rest times are all valid forms of progressive overload.
- **Progressing every single session:** As you become more advanced, you won't be able to add weight or reps every workout. Progression happens over weeks and months, not days.
