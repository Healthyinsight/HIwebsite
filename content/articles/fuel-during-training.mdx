The sports nutrition supplement industry generates billions annually selling products to athletes who don't need them — and failing to reach the athletes who do. Intra-workout fueling is genuinely evidence-supported in specific contexts. It's also genuinely unnecessary in others. Knowing the difference changes your training and your shopping cart.

## The Core Rule: Session Duration Determines Need

This is the most replicated finding in intra-workout nutrition research:

**Under 60 minutes**: Intra-workout carbohydrates provide no meaningful performance benefit for most people. Glycogen stores are sufficient. Water is all you need.

**60-90 minutes**: Benefit begins to appear, particularly if intensity is high or if you started the session with lower glycogen (e.g., fasted, or shortly after another session).

**Over 90 minutes**: Clear, consistent evidence for performance benefit. Carbohydrate delivery during exercise maintains blood glucose, spares muscle glycogen, and reduces perceived exertion.

**Over 2.5 hours**: Essential for sustained performance. Without carbohydrate intake, glycogen depletion causes the performance cliff commonly called "bonking" or "hitting the wall."

## What Happens When Glycogen Runs Out

Muscle glycogen is the primary fuel for moderate-to-high intensity exercise. The body stores approximately 400-600g of glycogen (1,600-2,400 kcal) between muscle and liver. At high intensities (70-80% VO2 max), this store depletes in approximately 90-120 minutes.

When glycogen falls critically low:
- Intensity must drop dramatically (the body shifts to fat oxidation, which is slower)
- Blood glucose can fall (hypoglycemia) — causing cognitive impairment, reduced decision-making, mood deterioration
- Power output and pace become impossible to maintain

Intra-workout carbohydrates maintain blood glucose and provide an exogenous energy source that reduces the rate of glycogen depletion — effectively extending the high-intensity window.

## The Right Amount: 30-60g of Carbs Per Hour

Multiple meta-analyses converge on 30-60g of carbohydrates per hour as the evidence-based range for endurance exercise over 60 minutes.

The key constraint: gut absorption capacity. Simple carbohydrates (glucose, fructose, maltodextrin) are absorbed by distinct intestinal transporters. Glucose absorption saturates at approximately 60g/hour through the SGLT1 transporter.

**Single-source carbohydrate (glucose/maltodextrin)**: Maximum absorption ~60g/hour
**Mixed-source carbohydrate (glucose + fructose, typically 2:1 ratio)**: Maximum absorption ~90g/hour by engaging both SGLT1 and GLUT5 transporters simultaneously

For events lasting 2-3+ hours at high intensity, the 2:1 glucose:fructose combination is evidence-supported for higher carbohydrate delivery rates. For training sessions, 30-60g/hour from any fast-digesting source is adequate.

## What Counts as Good Intra-Workout Carbs

Fast-digesting carbohydrates with low fiber and low fat are what you want:

**Evidence-based options (inexpensive and effective):**
- **Maltodextrin** (glucose polymer): absorbs rapidly, minimal gut issues, neutral taste, inexpensive
- **Sports drinks** with glucose/maltodextrin at 6-8% carbohydrate concentration: also provide fluid and electrolytes
- **Fruit-based options**: banana, dates, orange slices — natural, effective, slower absorption than pure glucose products
- **White rice or bread**: used by ultra-endurance athletes; slower than sports products but effective for very long efforts

**Commercial sports products:**
- **Energy gels**: concentrated (typically 20-25g carbohydrate per gel); effective when taken with water; practice gut tolerance in training
- **Energy chews**: similar to gels; easier to dose precisely; some people prefer solid texture
- **Sports drinks**: simultaneously address hydration and carbohydrate; easiest option for most training contexts

What matters is carbohydrate content and digestibility — not the brand or marketing claims on the packaging.

## Electrolytes: When They Matter

Electrolytes (primarily sodium) become relevant when:
- Session duration exceeds 90 minutes
- Sweat rate is high (hot weather, high intensity)
- You're a heavy or "salty" sweater (white residue on skin after exercise)

Sodium supports fluid retention and drives thirst — important for maintaining hydration over long sessions. The argument for electrolytes in short sessions is weak.

**Target sodium intake**: 500-700mg per hour of exercise in hot conditions; lower in cool weather and shorter sessions.

Most sports drinks contain adequate sodium. If using plain water and gels, add electrolyte tabs for sessions over 90 minutes in heat.

## Pre-Training Carbohydrate Matters More Than Intra-Workout

The most consistently overlooked intra-workout optimization is actually *before* the session. Glycogen status at the start of training determines both performance and the degree to which intra-workout fueling is needed.

**Fasted morning training** (glycogen partially depleted from overnight fast):
- Higher dependence on intra-workout carbohydrates for sessions over 45-60 minutes
- Fat oxidation is higher — which can be a deliberate adaptation target in base training but impairs high-intensity work quality

**Well-fueled training** (carbohydrate-containing meal 2-3 hours before):
- Full or near-full glycogen stores
- High-intensity work quality is better
- Intra-workout need is lower for sessions under 90 minutes

For most athletes, optimizing pre-training nutrition produces larger performance gains than obsessing over intra-workout products.

## Protein During Training: The Evidence

Intra-workout protein does not improve performance during the session. It also doesn't meaningfully accelerate within-session recovery. The evidence for protein during training is:

- **During sessions under 3 hours**: No performance benefit over carbohydrate alone
- **During ultra-endurance events (>3-4 hours)**: Some evidence for reducing muscle protein catabolism; marginal effect sizes
- **Co-ingestion with carbohydrate**: May reduce post-exercise muscle damage markers, but evidence is inconsistent

For most training contexts, intra-workout protein is neither necessary nor beneficial. Focus on post-workout protein instead.

## Practical Fueling Protocols

**For sessions under 60 minutes:**
- Hydration only (water, electrolytes if hot)
- No carbohydrate supplementation needed
- Exception: second session of the day within 8 hours; glycogen repletion between sessions is relevant

**For sessions 60-90 minutes:**
- 20-30g carbohydrates around the 60-minute mark (or continuously from 45 minutes for high-intensity sessions)
- One gel, half a banana, or 300-400ml sports drink

**For sessions 90-150 minutes:**
- 30-60g carbohydrates per hour from 30-45 minutes onward
- Practice gut tolerance — start with lower amounts and increase progressively

**For sessions 2.5+ hours:**
- 60-90g carbohydrates per hour using mixed glucose:fructose sources
- Sodium: 500-700mg/hour
- Practice fueling in training, never on race day

## The "Energy Crash" Problem

Energy crashes during moderate-duration training (30-60 minutes) are almost never caused by glycogen depletion — the stores are sufficient. More common causes:

1. **Starting the session with low blood sugar** (skipped breakfast or meal too early)
2. **Reactive hypoglycemia from pre-workout sugar** — high-glycemic foods 15-30 minutes before exercise can cause an insulin spike followed by a blood sugar drop at exercise onset
3. **Dehydration** — even mild dehydration (2% bodyweight) causes disproportionate fatigue perception
4. **Sleep deprivation** — perceived exertion is elevated and tolerance for discomfort reduced

The solution to most "energy crashes" is better pre-session nutrition and hydration, not intra-workout products.

## The Bottom Line

Intra-workout carbohydrates are one of the most evidence-supported ergogenic aids in sports nutrition — for the right sessions. They're also completely unnecessary in others.

The decision framework is simple: if the session is over 60 minutes at meaningful intensity, carbohydrate during the session will improve performance and reduce perceived effort. Under 60 minutes, water and a well-fueled pre-workout meal are sufficient.

Match your fueling to your training. Not to the marketing.
