You're 3 hours into a race. Your gels are gone. Your stomach is rebelling. Your legs still have fuel to give — but your gut is the bottleneck. Most athletes hit 60g/h and stop, not because their muscles are out of glycogen, but because of nausea, bloating, or cramps. The ceiling isn't in your legs. It's in how you've trained your gut.

## Here's the thing

Your intestine absorbs carbohydrates through dedicated transport proteins. Glucose uses SGLT1 — sodium-coupled glucose transporter 1. Fructose uses a completely separate channel: GLUT5. When you consume only glucose (from maltodextrin gels, for example), SGLT1 becomes saturated at around 60g/h. Add fructose, and GLUT5 opens in parallel. Two lanes, running simultaneously. Total absorption can reach 90g/h.[1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11901785/)[2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-014-0148-z)

In controlled trials, glucose-fructose combinations increase carbohydrate oxidation by 20–55% compared to glucose alone — with no increase in GI complaints when intake is trained.[1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11901785/)

## Here's what to do: 4 steps

**1. Match your carb ratio to your event duration**

For events under 2.5 hours, single-sugar products (30–60g/h) work well — there's no need to unlock the second transporter. For events over 2.5 hours, switch to a glucose-fructose blend targeting 60–90g/h:

- **2:1 glucose:fructose** — the classic, well-studied ratio (60g glucose + 30g fructose = 90g/h)
- **1:0.8 glucose:fructose** — newer evidence suggests this higher-fructose proportion may optimize oxidation efficiency[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01757-1)
- **1:1 ratio** — the practical middle ground; often easiest to find in consumer products

**2. Build your gut over 8 weeks**

Gut training progressively increases intestinal transporter capacity and reduces GI symptoms. Move through these stages during your long sessions:

- **Week 1–2:** 30g/h — establish your baseline with a product you tolerate
- **Week 3–4:** 45g/h — add one extra gel or serving per hour
- **Week 5–6:** 60g/h — switch to a dedicated 2:1 ratio product
- **Week 7–8:** 75–90g/h — race-simulation conditions, full protocol

A 2017 study confirmed that two weeks of repetitive gut challenge significantly increased carbohydrate absorption and reduced GI symptom frequency in trained runners.[4](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28244632/)

**3. Troubleshoot GI symptoms specifically**

Not all gut distress has the same fix:

- **Bloating** → reduce concentration, not volume — spread the same grams across more water
- **Nausea** → switch from gels to liquid or whole food; solid sources slow gastric emptying
- **Cramping** → increase sodium intake and temporarily reduce the fructose fraction

**4. Read your product labels**

Most sports gels use maltodextrin (a glucose polymer) as the primary ingredient. Some add fructose explicitly; others use sucrose, which is 50% fructose by weight. Know exactly what you're taking. "Sports drink + gel" combinations often land near the right ratio by accident — confirm the math before race day.

## What the research shows

The dual-transporter advantage was established when Jeukendrup and colleagues showed that glucose-fructose ingestion increased exogenous carbohydrate oxidation to around 1.7g/min — well above the ~1.0g/min ceiling for glucose alone.[1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11901785/)[2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-014-0148-z)

More recent work has refined the optimal ratio. A 2022 review by Podlogar and Wallis examined whether the traditional 2:1 ratio remains optimal or whether a higher fructose proportion could deliver superior oxidation.[3](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01757-1) For gut-trained athletes, the 1:0.8 ratio appears to improve total carbohydrate oxidation without increasing GI complaints — though it demands a more adapted gut to tolerate at high doses.

O'Brien and Rowlands found that a higher fructose-to-maltodextrin ratio improved exogenous carbohydrate oxidation in a controlled crossover trial, with GI tolerance holding when intake had been practiced consistently in training.[5](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23470300/)

## Deep dive

### How SGLT1 and GLUT5 work in parallel

SGLT1 is a sodium-coupled cotransporter: it moves glucose into intestinal epithelial cells by co-transporting sodium ions driven by a sodium gradient. This active process has a defined maximum rate — limited by transporter count and luminal concentration.

GLUT5 is a facilitated transporter: it moves fructose passively down its concentration gradient, independent of sodium. Crucially, SGLT1 and GLUT5 do not compete. They operate in parallel on adjacent cells in the intestinal lining. This is why mixing glucose and fructose doesn't create a bottleneck: you are filling two separate transport pipelines at the same time.

### Osmolality and why concentration matters

Hypertonic solutions — too concentrated in the stomach — delay gastric emptying and increase GI symptoms. Taking a gel without adequate water at 90g/h is a common error: the sugar concentration at the stomach exit overwhelms the gut's ability to manage fluid balance. Distributing carbohydrate across 400–600ml of water per hour keeps osmolality manageable and absorption smooth. Never mix gels and sports drink in the same bottle — the combined concentration will exceed what most athletes tolerate.

### Individual variation in transporter capacity

Transporter capacity varies significantly between individuals. Athletes with higher chronic training volume and longer histories of in-workout fueling consistently show higher carbohydrate oxidation rates and lower GI symptom rates at any given dose.[4](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28244632/) GLUT5 expression increases with regular fructose challenge, and intestinal transport adapts over weeks of consistent practice. This is why gut training is not optional for high-dose fueling strategies — you cannot absorb your way to 90g/h without training for it first.

## The bottom line

For events under 2.5 hours, standard 30–60g/h glucose-based products are sufficient. For longer efforts, the math changes: combining glucose and fructose in a 2:1 or 1:0.8 ratio unlocks 90g/h absorption — and 4–8 weeks of gut training is the bridge between knowing the protocol and actually tolerating it in a race.

**Start this week:** Check the labels on your current gels and drinks. Identify whether they contain fructose or sucrose (50% fructose by weight). For your next long session over 90 minutes, practice with 30–45g/h and build from there.

**The protocol in one line:** 30g/h in week 1, add 10–15g every two weeks, switch to a 2:1 blend at week 5, reach 75–90g/h by race week — and never try a new product or dose on race day.
