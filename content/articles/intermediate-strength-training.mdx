You've been training consistently for 6+ months. You track your workouts. You deload on schedule. But your squat hasn't moved in weeks, and you're not sure if it's your volume, frequency, or intensity that's the problem.

Sound familiar?

## Here's the thing

Intermediate training is not about trying harder. It's about controlling training variables in planned blocks so you can push, recover, and progress on purpose.

The practical move is simple:
- find your personal volume landmarks
- cycle volume and intensity through accumulation → intensification → realization
- deload deliberately, not accidentally

## Here's what to do: 5 steps

## 1) Find your volume landmarks (Weeks 1–4)

Before you "optimize" anything, you need a baseline for how much training you actually recover from.

Key concepts (per muscle group):
- **MEV (Minimum Effective Volume):** the lowest weekly sets that reliably drive progress
- **MRV (Maximum Recoverable Volume):** the highest weekly sets you can recover from without performance dropping
- **MV (Maintenance Volume):** the weekly sets that maintain strength

**4-week test**
- Week 1: 12 hard sets
- Week 2: 16 hard sets
- Week 3: 20 hard sets
- Week 4: deload at 8 hard sets

**How to interpret**
- If Week 1 shows no progress: MEV may be higher than 12 sets
- If Week 3 crushes recovery/performance: MRV is likely at or below that range
- Maintenance is usually lower than MRV (often around half as a starting estimate)

Track:
- performance (reps at a given effort)
- simple recovery markers (sleep, soreness, motivation)

## 2) Set a split that hits each muscle 2× per week

Once you know your weekly set targets, distribute them across the week.

Rule of thumb:
- train each muscle at least 2×/week

Frequency is a tool, not a religion. Pick the lowest frequency that lets you hit weekly volume with good technique and recovery.

## 3) Run a 9–12 week block cycle (accumulate → intensify → realize)

Blocks work because each phase has one clear job.

**Accumulation (4–6 weeks)**
- volume: high (near your recoverable limit)
- intensity: moderate (clean reps)
- reps: often 8–12

**Intensification (3–4 weeks)**
- volume: lower
- intensity: higher
- reps: often 4–6

**Realization (1 week)**
- volume: lower
- intensity: peak efforts (as appropriate)
- goal: express strength

**Deload (1 week)**
- reduce volume significantly
- keep some intensity, drop fatigue

## 4) Autoregulate with RPE (hit the target effort, not the target load)

RPE = Rate of Perceived Exertion (0–10). RIR = reps in reserve.

Rule:
- if the set was meant to be "hard but clean", adjust load or reps until it actually matches that effort

This helps you accumulate productive work while managing fatigue when life stress or sleep varies.

## 5) Review weekly, adjust every block

**Weekly check**
- total sets per muscle
- key lift performance at a given effort
- recovery markers (sleep, soreness, motivation)

**End-of-block adjustments (every 9–12 weeks)**
- if you get crushed in Weeks 3–4 of accumulation, your MRV estimate is too high → reduce next cycle
- if intensification is too easy, raise loads slightly next cycle
- if RPE is inconsistent, spend a block practicing tighter effort targets

## The bottom line

Intermediate progress isn't about heroics. It's about precision:
- your volume landmarks
- planned blocks
- fatigue management
- consistent review

When variables are controlled, plateaus become solvable.
