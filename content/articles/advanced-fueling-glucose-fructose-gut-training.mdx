You hit the 2-hour mark of your long ride or run, reach for your third energy gel, and your stomach immediately says "no." GI distress is the number one reason endurance athletes DNF. We're told to eat 90+ grams of carbs per hour to perform at our peak, but how do you actually absorb that much without your gut rebelling?

## Here's the thing

Your intestines have a speed limit. If you only consume glucose (maltodextrin), the transporters in your gut max out at about 60 grams per hour. To push beyond that and hit elite fueling targets, you must combine glucose with fructose to unlock a second transport pathway, and you must literally "train your gut" like a muscle to handle the load.

## Here's what to do

**The 2:1 Baseline:** For workouts under 2.5 hours, aim for 60–90 grams of carbohydrates per hour using a 2:1 ratio of glucose to fructose.

**The 1:0.8 Advanced Ratio:** For intense events lasting longer than 3 hours where you need 90–120g of carbs per hour, look for fueling products that use a 1:0.8 glucose-to-fructose ratio.

**Start Low and Build:** Do not attempt 90g/hr on race day. Start gut training 6–8 weeks out. Begin at 50g/hr during your long weekend sessions and increase by 10g every week.

**Hydrate Appropriately:** Highly concentrated carbs require water to empty from the stomach. Always chase gels with water, not electrolyte sports drinks, to avoid turning your stomach into a hypertonic brick.

## What the research shows

Scientific consensus shows that co-ingesting glucose and fructose increases total carbohydrate oxidation rates compared to glucose alone, allowing athletes to comfortably process >90g/h and significantly improving endurance performance. Furthermore, a 2023 systematic review concluded that dedicated "gut-training" — practicing high carbohydrate intake during exercise — reduces gastrointestinal discomfort and improves absorption capacity over time.

## Why it works

Carbohydrate absorption relies on specific intestinal transporters. Glucose uses the SGLT1 transporter, which becomes fully saturated at roughly 1 gram per minute (60g/hour). Fructose, however, uses a completely different transporter called GLUT5. By consuming a mix of both (like the classic 2:1 or the newer 1:0.8 ratio), you bypass the SGLT1 bottleneck and utilize both pathways simultaneously.

Gut training works because the intestines are highly adaptable. Regular exposure to high-carbohydrate loads during exercise physically increases the density and activity of these SGLT1 and GLUT5 transporters. Additionally, practicing your fueling strategy improves your stomach's gastric emptying rate, meaning the sugar and fluid leave your stomach and enter your intestines faster, reducing sloshing and nausea.

## Avoid these mistakes

- **Ignoring the ratio:** Eating a bag of pure glucose candy will cause GI distress once you pass 60g/hour. Always check the ingredients for a glucose/maltodextrin and fructose blend.
- **Underhydrating:** Consuming gels without water pulls fluid into your stomach from your bloodstream to dilute the heavy sugar concentration, causing severe cramps.
- **Waiting until race day:** Trying a new gel brand or jumping from 50g/hr in training to 100g/hr in a race is a guaranteed recipe for digestive disaster.
