Two of the most reliable sleep disruptors in competitive sport aren't training load or overtraining — they're competition nerves and travel. Both are predictable, which means they're manageable.

This guide covers what the evidence shows about pre-competition sleep disruption and how to minimize its performance impact, plus evidence-based strategies for maintaining sleep quality while traveling.

## Pre-Competition Sleep: What the Evidence Shows

Pre-competition insomnia is near-universal among competitive athletes. A survey of elite Australian athletes found that 64% reported sleep disturbance before competition, and 83% believed poor pre-competition sleep negatively affected their performance.

The good news: **the research suggests that a single poor night's sleep has less impact than athletes believe**.

### The Night-Before Effect

Several studies have examined performance after one night of poor sleep in otherwise rested athletes:

- Sprint performance: minimal impact (1-2%) after one poor night if preceding nights were adequate
- Technical performance: slightly more sensitive than simple power outputs
- Decision-making and reaction time: measurable decline with full sleep deprivation; minor with partial disruption

The key qualifier in all of this: **if preceding nights were adequate**. A poor competition eve following a week of short nights is much more damaging than a poor competition eve following a well-loaded sleep week.

### The Two-Nights-Before Rule

Consistent with the above, the most important sleep for competition performance is the night 2 nights before the event — not the final night. Athletes who slept well on night 2 but poorly on night 1 (competition eve) show similar performance to those who slept well on both nights.

**Practical implication:** If you know you'll sleep poorly the night before a big event, invest in maximizing the 2-5 nights before that. This is within your control; competition-eve sleep often isn't.

## Managing Pre-Competition Sleep Disruption

### Cognitive Strategies

Pre-competition insomnia is primarily driven by pre-sleep arousal — mental activation that keeps the system in alert mode. Evidence-based cognitive strategies:

**Worry time protocol**: Schedule 15-20 minutes earlier in the evening to write down all competition concerns and contingency plans. This prevents the rehearsal loop that drives arousal in bed. Getting concerns out of working memory onto paper reduces nocturnal rumination.

**Cognitive restructuring**: Replace catastrophic thoughts ("If I don't sleep I'll perform terribly") with accurate ones ("One poor night of sleep has minimal performance impact; I've trained for this"). Both statements are verifiable — and the accurate one reduces arousal.

**Acceptance-based approach**: Trying to force sleep increases arousal. Paradoxical intention (explicitly not trying to sleep, just resting quietly) reduces the performance anxiety around sleep itself.

### Behavioral Strategies

**Use the bed only for sleep**: Lying awake in bed for extended periods conditions the nervous system to associate the bed with wakefulness. If you're awake for more than 20 minutes, get up and do something calm until you feel sleepy.

**Sleep banking**: As described in the sleep extension article, load sleep in the 5-7 days before competition. The sleep debt-protected state is more resilient to a single disrupted night.

**Consistent pre-sleep routine**: Doing the same sequence of activities before bed primes the nervous system for sleep through conditioned association. This is particularly valuable when the environment changes (hotel rooms, unfamiliar locations).

### Pharmacological Tools

**Melatonin**: appropriate for circadian timing adjustment when traveling across time zones; not evidence-supported for managing competition-eve insomnia in people sleeping at an appropriate circadian time.

**Magnesium glycinate**: low-quality evidence for improving sleep quality; low risk; acceptable if it's part of an established routine before major changes.

**Sedative medications**: not recommended for competition contexts. Residual sedation effects can impair next-day performance, and dependency risk is real.

## Traveling for Competition: Sleep Management

Travel disrupts sleep through multiple mechanisms: time zone changes, unfamiliar environments, disrupted schedules, reduced sleep opportunity, and the stress of travel itself.

### Time Zone Management

The general rule for time zone adaptation: the body adjusts at approximately 1 time zone per day when traveling east, and 1.5 time zones per day when traveling west (eastward adaptation is harder because it requires advancing the clock — fighting against the natural tendency for the circadian rhythm to drift later).

**If traveling 1-3 hours east/west:**
- Minor adjustment; shift sleep timing 30-60 minutes in the direction of travel starting 2-3 days before departure
- Minimize caffeine use on travel day to avoid compounding circadian disruption

**If traveling 4+ hours east:**
- Strategic light exposure is most powerful: seek bright light in the morning at the destination (matching the local day)
- Melatonin (0.5-3mg) taken at local destination bedtime can accelerate eastward adjustment
- Avoid bright light in the late destination evening (which would delay your clock further)

**If traveling 4+ hours west:**
- Easier adaptation; stay awake to local bedtime at destination
- Seek afternoon/evening light in the days post-arrival
- Melatonin is less necessary for westward travel

### Hotel Room Optimization

Unfamiliar environments trigger higher vigilance (the "first night effect") — the brain keeps one hemisphere more active in unfamiliar surroundings, a likely evolutionary vestige. This predictably reduces deep sleep on the first night.

**Mitigating strategies:**
- Blackout curtains or travel eye mask (hotel rooms are often brighter than home environments)
- Earplugs or white noise (reduces the impact of unfamiliar sounds triggering vigilance)
- Bring a familiar pillow or pillowcase — olfactory familiarity has a genuine effect on perceived comfort
- Set room temperature to your home range (16-19°C if possible)

### Travel Day Sleep

Red-eye flights and overnight travel are sometimes unavoidable. Management:

- Neck pillow and eye mask for any sleep on transport
- Limit alcohol on flights — it disrupts sleep quality even in short bouts
- Use brief (20-30 minute) naps at destination if arriving at local daytime; avoid extended naps that will anchor you to home time

## Monitoring and Adaptation

Track subjective recovery each morning using a simple 1-10 scale for: sleep quality, energy levels, mood, and muscle soreness. Four consistent questions give a better picture than any single metric.

If scores are consistently low in the days before competition, the intervention is more sleep (banking) and stress management — not more training. Performance preparation the week before a competition is completed; what remains is consolidation.

## The Bottom Line

Pre-competition sleep disruption is nearly universal and its performance impact is smaller than athletes believe — provided the preceding sleep week was well-managed. The actionable focus is on the nights you can control (5-7 days before the event) rather than the night you can't (competition eve).

For travel, circadian management via light exposure and melatonin, combined with simple environmental optimization, allows most athletes to maintain sleep quality within a competitive range.
